## Week 1 · Establish the thesis

### Post 1 — TEARDOWN
**Signal:** Depot charging NOI — field observation
**Graphic:** Dark navy card, white stat ("X% of sites" style)

---

The honest version of most depot charging business cases I've seen: the maths doesn't work.

Not because the tech's wrong.
Because nobody thought through how it would actually run.

The grid connection gets confirmed after the chargers are ordered.
The tariff gets negotiated after the substation's in.
The ops team finds out they own it at commissioning.

Then everyone's surprised the NOI doesn't close.

The sites that actually work do the boring stuff first — grid capacity, tariff structure, operations model — before a single piece of hardware gets specified.

Turns out boring is load-bearing.

### Post 2 — THESIS POST
**Signal:** Decade Energy €22M raise + Kristofer Fröjd's BESS thesis
**Graphic:** Two-column comparison card (old model vs. new model)

---

Decade Energy just raised €22M to build energy infrastructure at logistics depots.

The model: zero CapEx for operators. Decade owns the power, storage, and charging stack. You just use it.

I've seen a lot of infrastructure finance models. This one's interesting because it solves a problem most people don't name directly.

The problem isn't capital.
It's that most operators don't want to be in the energy business — they want to run logistics. The zero-CapEx model lets them not be in the energy business.

The e-truck TCO is falling fast. Chinese OEM pressure is real. BESS is getting better every year.

The operators who will actually capture that value aren't necessarily the ones with the most charge points. They're the ones who figured out what they own and what they hand to someone else.

Finance model and operating model need to be designed together.
Most programmes treat them as separate decisions.
That's where it goes sideways.

#DepotEnergy #LogisticsElectrification #EVTrucks

## Week 2 · Real market signals

### Post 3 — THESIS POST
**Signal:** Shell Recharge divestment (confirm still current before publishing)
**Graphic:** Timeline card — "what happened and when"

---

Shell Recharge is selling down its public charging network.

Worth understanding why, because it's not really a Shell story.

Public EV charging looked like infrastructure in 2020. High CapEx, long returns, but infrastructure-grade predictability.

Turned out it's not infrastructure. It's retail.
Utilisation risk. Competitor pricing. Demand that's lumpy and hard to forecast.
The return profile looks nothing like a pipeline or a grid connection.

The operators who are surviving did one of two things.
They went behind-the-meter — depot, fleet, private — where you know who's charging and roughly when.
Or they went ultra-rapid and built for peak demand, not average throughput.

Shell's not the problem. The original model was the problem.
Everyone who built public charging on infrastructure assumptions is stress-testing that model right now.

Some are rebuilding it. Some are selling out. Either way, the sector is getting smarter about what public charging actually is.

#EVCharging #ChargingInfrastructure #EnergyTransition

### Post 4 — FRAMEWORK
**Signal:** Matthijs van den Heuvel's shared HDC question
**Graphic:** Three-step breakdown — "where it breaks"

---

Every shared depot charging conversation I've had this year falls apart at the same point.

Not the hardware. Not the grid.
The commercial model.

A supplier wants to use your depot charger.
Sounds simple. Here's where it actually gets interesting.

The lease wasn't written for third-party access. Renegotiating takes longer than anyone expects.

The metering question doesn't have a standard answer yet. Whose meter? Whose tariff? Who holds the imbalance risk? Every site figures this out from scratch.

The utilisation case doesn't close at site level. You need network-level demand aggregation to make the economics work. One site can't deliver that alone.

The technology for shared heavy-duty charging is there.
The operating framework isn't — not in any standardised form.

The operators who figure this out first will have an edge that's genuinely hard to copy, because it's not in the hardware.

#HeavyDutyCharging #DepotCharging #FleetElectrification

## Week 3 · Operator depth

### Post 5 — TEARDOWN
**Signal:** Prologis estate — behind-the-meter observations
**Graphic:** Dark navy card with three numbered insights

---

Three things I didn't understand about depot electrification until I was inside it.

One: the design problem is peak demand, not average load.
Most specifications get built around average throughput.
But eHGV charging is a peak problem — eight trucks at shift end, all plugging in simultaneously.
The hardware handles it. The grid contract often doesn't.
You find this out after commissioning. Not ideal.

Two: BESS changes the economics more than the spec suggests.
It's not just resilience. It's the difference between a demand tariff that works and one that doesn't.
Operators who got storage right early are holding margin that others can't replicate now.
The ones who added it as an afterthought are retrofitting infrastructure that wasn't designed for it.

Three: commissioning is where programme debt matures.
Every shortcut taken in mobilisation lands at commissioning.
Most programmes don't have a named commissioning owner.
They have a contractor and a handover checklist. That's different.

None of this is complicated in hindsight.
All of it was expensive to learn the hard way.

#eHGV #DepotCharging #BehindTheMeter #BESS

### Post 6 — FRAMEWORK
**Signal:** 15 years of programme patterns
**Graphic:** Four gates visual — numbered, brand colours

---

Every electrification programme I've seen stall does it at one of four points.

Gate 1 is grid.
The connection was assumed, not confirmed.
Lead times weren't in the plan. The hardware's ready. The grid isn't.
[X-month] delays from this alone are normal. Nobody budgets for it.

Gate 2 is the commercial model.
The revenue case was built on forecast utilisation.
Month six actual is half of that.
Nobody owns the shortfall.

Gate 3 is operations handover.
The construction contractor finished on time.
The operations team wasn't ready.
No SOP, no trained people, no escalation path.
The programme delivered the asset and walked away.

Gate 4 is scale.
Site one worked. Site two took three times as long.
The programme was built for proof of concept, not replication.

Most operators hit all four.
The ones who don't design for them before mobilisation.

Which gate is yours at right now?

#Electrification #ProgrammeDelivery #EVInfrastructure

### Post 8 — THESIS POST
**Signal:** Kristofer Fröjd's BESS economics thesis + Nordic flexibility market
**Graphic:** Stacked bar showing "anchor vs upside" revenue mix

---

The BESS merchant business is going away.

Not disappearing — repricing. The easy arbitrage window has closed. First movers captured those returns. Every project model built on merchant revenue is getting stress-tested right now.

What I'm seeing replace it: behind-the-meter, with grid services on top.

The difference matters.

Merchant BESS is built around price spread arbitrage. When that spread compresses, the model breaks.

Behind-the-meter depot BESS is built around avoiding a demand tariff that would otherwise kill the depot economics. That value is predictable. It's there regardless of what wholesale prices do.

The grid services revenue is real, but it's the upside — not the anchor.

Most projects I see are building these in the wrong order. They're designing for the flexibility market and hoping the depot economics work out.

The operators who hold margin over the next five years designed the demand tariff avoidance first.
Everything else is a bonus.

#BESS #FlexibilityMarket #DepotEnergy #BehindTheMeter
