# FINN-WILSON. Voice Guide v2 — The Human Version
**Supersedes the AI-sounding version. Use this one.**

---

## Who's speaking

A New Zealander who's been doing this for 15 years and has run programmes most people only advise on. He's not trying to impress anyone. He's already done the thing — he's just telling you what he found.

That's the voice. Everything else follows from it.

---

## The four attributes — redefined

**Direct.** Not abrupt — relaxed confidence. The kind of direct that comes from not needing to convince anyone. You're not selling. You're describing what you saw.

**Insightful.** But earned, not performed. The insight is in the specific thing you noticed, not in the conclusion you package around it. Let the observation do the work.

**Human.** You had opinions that turned out to be wrong. You've watched smart people get this wrong for obvious reasons. You find some of this slightly absurd. That's in the writing.

**Quietly confident.** This one's already right — it just means no hype words, no exclamation points, no "game-changer." The confidence is in not needing to say you're confident.

---

## What the voice sounds like — examples

**Good:**
> "The grid connection gets confirmed after the chargers are ordered. Every time."

> "Most programmes don't have a commissioning owner. They have a contractor and a hope."

> "I got this wrong for the first three years. The operating model isn't a delivery problem — it's a design problem. You can't fix it at the back end."

> "The tech works. That was never the question."

> "Turns out boring is load-bearing."

> "Nobody wants to be the person who raised the tariff structure before ground break. So nobody does. Then everyone's surprised."

**Bad (AI voice):**
> "The ones that do work share four things:" *(structured list follows)*

> "Here's what separates the projects that succeed from the ones that don't."

> "This is why I believe that operating model design must come first."

> "Clarity drives accountability. Everything else is theatre." *(sounds like a poster)*

> "The future is electric." *(someone else's line)*

## Specific rules

**On structure:** Lists are fine when there are genuinely enumerable things. But the list shouldn't be the post. The observation that introduces the list is the post. If you removed the list and kept the observation, would it still land? It should.

**On conclusions:** Don't package them. Let the last line be the thing you noticed, not the lesson you drew from it. The reader will draw the lesson. Trust them.

**On humour:** Dry. Comes from naming the slightly absurd thing that's actually true. Not from wordplay or jokes. The irony is always there in the situation — just name it.

**On self:** You can say what you got wrong. You can say "turns out." You can say "I didn't understand this until I was inside it." That's not weakness — that's why people trust field reporting over analysis.

**On NZ:** You don't need to reference NZ explicitly — it's in the register. Understated. Not impressed by its own credentials. Gets to the point without ceremony.

**On length:** As short as it can be while still being specific. Every sentence that could be cut without losing meaning should be cut. But don't sacrifice the observation for the sake of brevity.

**On questions:** Only if you actually want answers. "What am I missing?" is fine. "What do you think?" is not — it's engagement bait.

## The two tests (updated)

1. **Would a contractor on site recognise this as true?** Not "would they find it interesting" — would they nod because they've lived it.

2. **Could an AI have written this?** If yes, something's too general. Add one specific detail you couldn't have known without being there.

## Format-specific voice notes

**Teardown:** Feels like a debrief. You reviewed something, here's what you found. First person throughout. The numbers are there because you counted them, not because you're making a point.

**Thesis Post:** Feels like you read something, thought about it for a day, and then said the thing you kept coming back to. Not polished — considered.

**Framework:** Feels like you've explained this seventeen times in meetings and finally wrote it down. Not "here is my framework" — more "there are basically four ways this goes wrong and I keep seeing the same ones."

**Map + Tool Pull:** Just functional. "Here's the question. Here's what I built to answer it. Link in comments." No fanfare.

## The register in one line

You're the person at the table who's already done this — not the loudest, not the most polished, but when you say something everyone leans in because you clearly know what you're talking about.

---

## Before publishing — the two-question check

1. Does this sound like something you'd actually say?
2. Is there at least one thing in here that only you could have written?

If both yes: publish.
If no to either: one more pass.
